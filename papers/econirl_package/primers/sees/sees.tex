\section{Problem and Motivation}
%% ============================================================

The nested fixed point algorithm (NFXP) of \citet{rust1987} solves the Bellman equation to machine precision at every candidate parameter vector, costing $O(|\calS|^2)$ per inner iteration. The CCP estimator of \citet{hotzMiller1993} eliminates the inner loop but requires an $O(|\calS|^3)$ matrix inversion. Both methods scale with the full state space. When $|\calS|$ reaches hundreds or thousands of states, as in multi-component replacement models or empirical games, the computational cost becomes prohibitive.

\citet{luoSang2024} propose the Sieve-based Efficient Estimator for Structural models (SEES). The value function is approximated by a small set of deterministic basis functions, and the Bellman equation is imposed as a penalty rather than a constraint. This produces a single unconstrained optimization over the structural parameters $\theta$ and basis coefficients $\alpha$, with computational cost $O(K)$ independent of $|\calS|$. No inner loop is solved. No neural network is trained. When $K = |\calS|$ and the penalty weight goes to infinity, SEES reduces to the MPEC estimator of \citet{suJudd2012}.

The sieve approximation replaces $V(s)$ with
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sieve_v}
V(s;\alpha) = \Psi(s)^\top \alpha,
\end{equation}
where $\Psi(s) = (\psi_1(s), \ldots, \psi_K(s))^\top$ is a deterministic basis. For Fourier bases, $\Psi(s) = (1, \cos(\pi \tilde s), \sin(\pi \tilde s), \cos(2\pi \tilde s), \ldots)$ with $\tilde s \in [0,1]$. Substituting into the Bellman equation gives approximate action values $Q(s,a;\theta,\alpha) = \theta^\top \phi(s,a) + \beta\, (P_a \Psi)^\top \alpha$, where $P_a \Psi$ is precomputed once per action.

The penalized criterion jointly maximizes the log-likelihood and penalizes Bellman violations:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:criterion}
\max_{\theta, \alpha} \; \sum_{i=1}^N \log \pi(a_i \mid s_i; \theta, \alpha) \;-\; \omega \sum_{s} \bigl(V(s;\alpha) - T_\sigma(V;\theta)(s)\bigr)^2.
\end{equation}


%% ============================================================
\section{Core Results}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Theorem 1 (Sieve Consistency).}
Under regularity conditions P1--P7 of \citet{luoSang2024}, as $\omega = \omega_n \to \infty$ with $n$, the sieve approximation converges uniformly to the exact equilibrium mapping: $\sup_{\theta \in \Theta} \|\hat p(\theta) - p^*(\theta)\|_2 = o_P(1)$. The penalty forces the approximate value function toward the Bellman fixed point. Once the penalty is strong enough, the data likelihood drives the structural parameter estimates. The critical rate condition is $\omega_n / n^2 \to \infty$: the penalty must grow faster than the statistical error, but not so fast that the sieve coefficients $\alpha$ are over-constrained before the data has shaped them.

\paragraph{Theorem 3 (Asymptotic Normality and Efficiency).}
If $\omega_n / n^2 \to \infty$ and regularity conditions P1--P9 hold, the SEES estimator satisfies
\[
\sqrt{n}(\tilde\theta_n - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \Sigma), \qquad \Sigma = -V_{\theta_0}^{-1},
\]
attaining the Cram\'er-Rao lower bound. SEES is asymptotically efficient despite never solving the Bellman equation.

\paragraph{Corollary 3.1 (Schur Complement SEs).}
The joint Hessian over $(\theta, \alpha)$ has a block structure. The marginal Hessian for $\theta$ is the Schur complement
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:schur}
\tilde H_\theta = H_{\theta\theta} - H_{\theta\alpha}\, H_{\alpha\alpha}^{-1}\, H_{\alpha\theta}.
\end{equation}
Standard errors from $(-\tilde H_\theta)^{-1}$ marginalize out the basis coefficients and give the correct Fisher information for the structural parameters. Unlike NNES, which achieves a block-diagonal information matrix via the zero Jacobian property, SEES requires the full Schur complement because the off-diagonal blocks $H_{\theta\alpha}$ are generally nonzero.

\paragraph{Relationship to MPEC.}
When $K = |\calS|$ and $\omega \to \infty$, the sieve expansion becomes exact and the penalty enforces $V = T_\sigma(V;\theta)$ as a hard constraint. In this limit, SEES is the MPEC estimator of \citet{suJudd2012}, which jointly optimizes $(\theta, V)$ subject to the Bellman equation. SEES generalizes MPEC by allowing $K \ll |\calS|$, reducing the optimization from $d_\theta + |\calS|$ unknowns to $d_\theta + K$ unknowns at the cost of a controlled approximation error.

\paragraph{Projection bias.}
If the value function has sharp discontinuities that the basis cannot represent, the resulting projection bias contaminates $\hat\theta$. The paper recommends increasing $K$ until parameter estimates stabilize. Smooth value functions are well-approximated by Fourier or polynomial bases at moderate $K$. In the multi-component bus engine, the value function is a smooth function of summed mileage, so a Fourier basis with $K = 8$ terms captures it well.

\section{Estimation Algorithm}
%% ============================================================

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\DontPrintSemicolon
\caption{SEES: Joint Algorithm \citep{luoSang2024}}
\label{alg:sees}
\KwIn{Panel $\{(s_i, a_i)\}_{i=1}^N$, features $\phi(s,a)$, transitions $P(s'|s,a)$, discount $\beta$, scale $\sigma$, basis type, dimension $K$, penalty $\omega$}
\BlankLine

\tcp{Step 1: Build sieve basis}
$\Psi \leftarrow$ basis matrix of shape $(|\calS|, K)$\;
Normalize states to $[0,1]$ (Fourier) or $[-1,1]$ (polynomial)\;
\BlankLine

\tcp{Step 2: Precompute continuation values}
\For{each action $a$}{
    $\E_a[\Psi] \leftarrow P_a \cdot \Psi$ \tcp*{shape $(|\calS|, K)$, one matrix multiply}
}
\BlankLine

\tcp{Step 3: Define penalized criterion (Eq.~\ref{eq:criterion})}
$x = [\theta, \alpha] \in \R^{d_\theta + K}$\;
$f(x) = \sum_i \log \pi(a_i | s_i; \theta, \alpha) - \omega \sum_s (V(s;\alpha) - T_\sigma(V;\theta)(s))^2$\;
\BlankLine

\tcp{Step 4: Joint L-BFGS-B optimization}
$\hat x \leftarrow \arg\max_x f(x)$ \tcp*{single call, autodiff gradient}
$\hat\theta, \hat\alpha \leftarrow \hat x[:d_\theta],\; \hat x[d_\theta:]$\;
\BlankLine

\tcp{Step 5: Standard errors via Schur complement (Eq.~\ref{eq:schur})}
$H \leftarrow$ numerical Hessian of $f$ at $\hat x$\;
$\tilde H_\theta \leftarrow H_{\theta\theta} - H_{\theta\alpha}\, H_{\alpha\alpha}^{-1}\, H_{\alpha\theta}$\;
$\text{SE}(\hat\theta) \leftarrow \sqrt{\text{diag}(-\tilde H_\theta^{-1})}$\;
\BlankLine

\KwOut{$\hat\theta$, $V^*$, $\pi^*$, $\text{SE}(\hat\theta)$, log-likelihood}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Implementation.}
The entire estimation is a single L-BFGS-B call over the $(d_\theta + K)$-dimensional vector. JAX autodiff computes the gradient through the penalized log-likelihood, including the softmax policy and the soft Bellman operator. The precomputed $\E_a[\Psi] = P_a \Psi$ matrices make continuation value evaluation a matrix-vector multiply, avoiding any inner loop or fixed-point iteration. Bounds on $\alpha$ (default $[-50, 50]$) prevent the basis coefficients from diverging during optimization.

\paragraph{Smoothing parameter selection.}
\citet{luoSang2024} recommend starting with a moderate $\omega$ and multiplying by a factor $L$ until the confidence intervals from successive values of $\omega$ overlap by more than a threshold. In practice, values between 1 and 100 work well. At low $\omega$ the data shapes the value function freely, producing a good starting point. At high $\omega$ the Bellman constraint dominates, pushing the estimator toward MLE. The paper proves that any $\omega$ satisfying $\omega_n / n^2 \to \infty$ yields an asymptotically efficient estimator.

\section{Results}
%% ============================================================

\input{sees_results}

We run SEES and NFXP on a \seesKcomp-component bus engine with \seesNstates\ total states (\seesMbins\ mileage bins per component), discount $\beta = \seesBeta$, and \seesNobs\ observations. SEES uses a Fourier basis with $K = 8$ terms. This environment tests the scaling argument from \citet{luoSang2024}: with $|\calS| = \seesNstates$, NFXP must solve a $\seesNstates \times \seesNstates$ Bellman fixed point at each parameter guess, while SEES optimizes over $d_\theta + 8 = 11$ unknowns regardless of $|\calS|$.

SEES achieves a log-likelihood within \seesLLgap\ nats of NFXP while running \seesSpeedup$\times$ faster (\seesTime s vs \seesNfxpTime s). The speedup comes entirely from eliminating the inner fixed-point iteration: at $\beta = \seesBeta$, the Bellman contraction rate is $(1 - \beta)$, requiring tens of thousands of value iteration steps per NFXP inner call. SEES bypasses this entirely.

Both estimators agree closely on the replacement cost parameter. The operating and quadratic cost parameters show larger discrepancies because their effects on the likelihood surface are small relative to replacement cost, making them harder to identify precisely. The key observation is that the log-likelihoods are nearly identical: SEES finds the same statistical optimum as NFXP despite optimizing over only 11 unknowns ($d_\theta + K = 3 + 8$) instead of solving a $\seesNstates \times \seesNstates$ fixed point.

SEES occupies a middle ground between MPEC and NNES. The basis functions are deterministic and require no training, making the optimization smooth and well-conditioned. The trade-off is projection bias when $V$ is not well-represented by the basis. NNES \citep{nguyen2025} avoids this with a universal approximator and achieves valid SEs via Neyman orthogonality, at the cost of alternating optimization and sensitivity to initialization.

The key implementation details map directly to Algorithm~\ref{alg:sees}: \texttt{\_build\_basis()} constructs $\Psi$ (Step 1), the \texttt{penalized\_ll()} closure implements Equation~\ref{eq:criterion} (Step 3), and the Schur complement in Equation~\ref{eq:schur} is computed from the numerical Hessian (Step 5).
