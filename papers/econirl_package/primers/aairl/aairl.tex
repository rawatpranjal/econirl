\section{Problem and Motivation}
%% ============================================================

Standard IRL methods recover a single reward function from observed behavior, but content platforms and subscription services host consumers with fundamentally different tastes. A quality-loving user who buys every episode and a price-sensitive user who exits early have nothing in common structurally, and fitting one model to the pooled data recovers a meaningless average that misrepresents both types. AIRL \citep{fu2018} addresses reward ambiguity from potential-based shaping but assumes a homogeneous population and requires state-only rewards, making it inapplicable to dynamic discrete choice models with exit actions and absorbing states. \citet{lee2026} extend AIRL with anchor constraints that identify action-dependent rewards and an EM algorithm that recovers latent consumer segments. We call this Anchored AIRL (AAIRL).

%% ============================================================
\section{Model and Notation}
%% ============================================================

\commonnotation

Each consumer belongs to one of $K$ latent segments. Segment $k$ has reward function $r^*_k(s,a)$ and solves the maximum causal entropy problem
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mce}
\max_\pi \; \E_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \beta^t \big(r_k(s_t, a_t) + \sigma\, \calH(\pi(\cdot \mid s_t))\big)\right],
\end{equation}
giving the softmax optimal policy $\pi^*_k(a \mid s) = \exp(A^*_k(s,a)/\sigma) / Z_k(s)$ where $A^*_k = Q^*_k - V^*_k$.

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}
\FloatBarrier

The AIRL discriminator decomposes the logit into reward and shaping:
\begin{align}
f^k_{\theta,\phi}(s,a,s') &= g_\theta^k(s,a) + \beta\, h_\phi^k(s') - h_\phi^k(s), \label{eq:f}\\[3pt]
D^k_{\theta,\phi}(s,a,s') &= \frac{\exp(f^k_{\theta,\phi})}{\exp(f^k_{\theta,\phi}) + \pi^k(a \mid s)}. \label{eq:D}
\end{align}
Each trajectory $\tau_i$ is generated by consumer $i$ who draws segment $k$ with probability $\lambda_k$ and then follows policy $\pi^*_k$ across all their series. Consumers are assigned consistent segment membership across multiple series via a consistency constraint on the posteriors.

%% ============================================================
\section{Identification}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Reward shaping ambiguity.} IRL rewards are identified only up to potential-based shaping \citep{ng1999}: for any $h : \calS \to \R$, the shaped reward $r'(s,a) = r(s,a) + \beta h(s') - h(s)$ induces the same optimal policy. The observed policy constrains $|\calS|(|\calA|-1)$ equations while the reward has $|\calS| \cdot |\calA|$ unknowns, leaving $|\calS|$ free variables \citep{cao2021}.

\paragraph{Standard AIRL \citep[Theorem~1]{fu2018}.} At the adversarial optimum, if the reward is state-only and the MDP is decomposable (all states recurrent), then $g_\theta(s) = r^*(s) + c$. The decomposability condition excludes absorbing states; state-only rewards exclude action-dependent utilities. Both assumptions fail in DDC.

\paragraph{Anchor identification \citep{lee2026}.} Two normalization anchors eliminate the $|\calS|$ residual degrees of freedom and extend the result to action-dependent rewards. If
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:anchors}
g_\theta^k(s,\, a_{\text{exit}}) = 0\ \forall s, \qquad h_\phi^k(s_{\text{abs}}) = 0,
\end{equation}
then at the adversarial optimum $g_\theta^k(s,a) = r^*_k(s,a)$ and $h_\phi^k(s) = V^*_k(s)$. The exit-action anchor pins the reward level; the absorbing-state anchor pins the value level. Together they uniquely identify the full action-dependent reward.

\paragraph{EM identifiability.} With $K \geq 2$ segments and prior $\lambda$, the mixture model is locally identifiable when segment policies $\pi^*_k$ are distinct \citep{yakowitz1968}. The EM algorithm recovers the correct segments when initialization is diversified across segments via small random perturbations.

\section{Estimation Algorithm}
%% ============================================================

\begin{algorithm}[h!]
\caption{EM-AIRL: Anchored AIRL with Latent Heterogeneity \citep{lee2026}}
\label{alg:aairl}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Expert trajectories $\{\tau_i\}_{i=1}^N$, segments $K$, discount $\beta$,
  anchor action $a_{\text{exit}}$, absorbing state $s_{\text{abs}}$, learning rate $\alpha$
\ENSURE Segment rewards $\{g_\theta^k\}_{k=1}^K$, priors $\{\lambda_k\}$, posteriors $\{q_{ik}\}$

\STATE Initialize $g_\theta^k$ with small random perturbations; enforce anchors~\eqref{eq:anchors}
\STATE Initialize $\lambda_k = 1/K$,\; $q_{ik} = 1/K$ for all $i, k$

\FOR{EM iteration $= 1, 2, \ldots$ until convergence}

  \STATE \textbf{E-step:} For each trajectory $\tau_i$ and segment $k$:
  \STATE \quad Compute $\ell_{ik} \leftarrow \log \lambda_k + \sum_t \log \pi^k(a_t \mid s_t)$
  \STATE \quad Set $q_{ik} \leftarrow \exp(\ell_{ik}) / \sum_{k'} \exp(\ell_{ik'})$ \quad (log-sum-exp stable)
  \STATE \quad Apply within-user consistency: average $q_{ik}$ across trajectories from same individual

  \STATE \textbf{Update priors:} $\lambda_k \leftarrow \bigl(\tfrac{1}{N}\sum_i q_{ik} + \varepsilon_{\text{smooth}}\bigr)$, renormalize

  \FOR{segment $k = 1, \ldots, K$}
    \FOR{AIRL round $= 1, \ldots, J$}
      \STATE Sample $M$ transitions $\calD_{\text{sim}}$ from current segment-$k$ policy $\pi^k$
      \FOR{discriminator step $= 1, \ldots, d$}
        \STATE $\calL_{\text{disc}} \leftarrow -\frac{1}{N}\sum_i q_{ik} \sum_{\calD_i}\log D^k - \frac{1}{M}\sum_{\calD_{\text{sim}}}\log(1-D^k)$
        \STATE $g_\theta^k, h_\phi^k \leftarrow$ Adam step on $\calL_{\text{disc}}$
        \STATE Enforce anchors~\eqref{eq:anchors} on $g_\theta^k$
      \ENDFOR
      \STATE Update $\pi^k$ via soft value iteration on $g_\theta^k$ (conservative mixing)
      \IF{$\|\pi^k_{\text{new}} - \pi^k_{\text{old}}\|_\infty < \varepsilon_{\text{AIRL}}$} \textbf{break} \ENDIF
    \ENDFOR
  \ENDFOR

  \STATE Compute mixture LL: $\calL = \sum_i \log \sum_k \lambda_k \prod_t \pi^k(a_t \mid s_t)$
  \IF{$|\Delta \calL| / |\calL| < \varepsilon_{\text{EM}}$} \textbf{break} \ENDIF

\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
\FloatBarrier

\paragraph{Conservative mixing.} The tabular policy generator uses $\pi^k_{\text{new}} = (1 - \alpha_p)\,\pi^k_{\text{old}} + \alpha_p\,\pi^{\text{VI}}$ with $\alpha_p \leq 0.2$ to prevent reward divergence from exact value iteration overshooting. The anchors~\eqref{eq:anchors} are enforced after every discriminator update.

\paragraph{Convergence.} The outer EM loop terminates when the relative change in mixture log-likelihood falls below $\varepsilon_{\text{EM}} = 0.001$. The inner AIRL loop terminates when $\|\pi^k_{\text{new}} - \pi^k_{\text{old}}\|_\infty < \varepsilon_{\text{AIRL}} = 10^{-4}$.

\section{Simulation}
%% ============================================================

We construct a 51-state serialized content MDP (50 episode states plus one absorbing terminal state, 3 actions: buy, wait, exit, 6 linear reward features). Two latent consumer types are drawn from ground truth: Segment~A (``Heavy Reader,'' 55\% prior) values quality strongly with parameters $[-0.2,\, 2.5,\, 1.2,\, -0.4,\, 0.8,\, 0.6]$; Segment~B (``Price-Sensitive,'' 45\% prior) has high price sensitivity with parameters $[-1.8,\, 0.4,\, 0.3,\, -0.1,\, 0.1,\, 0.2]$. Data consist of \aairlNusers{} users reading 3 books each, producing \aairlNobs{} observations.

\input{aairl_results}

The table tells the story directly. AAIRL~$K{=}2$ assigns \aairlSegAcc{} of trajectories to the correct segment, recovers Segment~A with cosine similarity \aairlCosA{} and Segment~B with similarity \aairlCosB{}. Within each type, the segment-specific policy matches observed behavior \aairlPaccA{} and \aairlPaccB{} of the time. AIRL~$K{=}1$ fits a single reward vector to the pooled data; the cosine similarity between that vector and the true-average reward is \airlKoneCos{}, but this average reward misrepresents both types and prevents any segment-specific analysis or counterfactual. BC matches surface action frequencies (\bcAcc{} accuracy) but encodes no structural information and cannot recover unobserved types.

The decisive advantage of AAIRL is identifiability. It is the only estimator in this comparison that recovers two distinct reward functions from a mixed population. A downstream analyst who wants to understand why Segment~B exits early, or simulate the effect of a pricing intervention targeted at price-sensitive users, can only answer that question with an estimator that discovers the segment. Run \texttt{python aairl\_run.py} in this directory to regenerate all results.
