# Benchmark protocol for econirl

This protocol specifies what to report and what to track in every cross-estimator benchmark in the paper, the documentation, and any future paper that compares estimators in econirl. The goal is that any reader of any benchmark table in this package can answer four questions without leaving the page. Did the estimator recover the parameters? Did the estimator recover a useful policy? How long did it take? Can I reproduce the result?

The protocol is consciously borrowed from three traditions. The reproducibility crisis literature in deep reinforcement learning, summarized in Henderson et al. (2018) and operationalized in Stable-Baselines3, drives the seed-and-hardware reporting requirements. The structural econometrics tradition, summarized in Iskhakov et al. (2016) and operationalized in PyBLP, drives the identification diagnostics and convergence reporting requirements. The benchmarking literature in machine learning, operationalized in scikit-learn and OpenAI Gym, drives the dataset-and-environment registration requirements.

## What to report in every benchmark table

Every cross-estimator benchmark table in the paper or in `examples/` must include the following columns. Missing values are reported as a dash, never as a blank. Columns marked optional may be omitted only when they do not apply to the experiment.

1. **Estimator name.** The class name as imported, written in code font.
2. **Family.** Structural, sieve, neural, max-entropy IRL, Q-function IRL, adversarial IRL, distribution IRL, or imitation. The label drawn from `econirl.estimation.categories.EstimatorCategory`.
3. **Point estimate per parameter.** One column per parameter when there are at most three. A summary norm such as the Euclidean distance to ground truth when there are more.
4. **Standard error per parameter.** Same convention as point estimate. The method used (asymptotic, robust, bootstrap, clustered) is named in the table caption.
5. **Maximized log-likelihood.** Reported to two decimal places. For IRL estimators that do not maximize a likelihood, report the value of the estimator's own objective and label the column accordingly.
6. **Wall-clock time.** Total time from the call to `estimate` to the return, in seconds. Reported to one decimal place below ten seconds, to the nearest integer above.
7. **Convergence flag.** A boolean. False means the estimator hit its iteration cap or returned a non-positive-definite Hessian.
8. **Cosine similarity to ground truth.** Required when ground truth is known, as in simulated experiments and replications. Reported to four decimal places. Computed on the parameter vector. Replaces RMSE and bias as the primary scale-invariant accuracy measure for IRL where rewards are identified only up to constants.
9. **Percent optimal value.** The value achieved by the estimated policy as a fraction of the value achieved by the optimal policy under the data-generating process. Required when ground truth is known. Reported as a percentage with one decimal place.
10. **Percent transfer value.** The same metric but computed on a held-out transition structure with the same reward. Required for IRL benchmarks because the whole point of recovering a reward is transferability. Optional for pure structural benchmarks.

## What to report in every Monte Carlo experiment

A Monte Carlo experiment is a benchmark with R replications under a known data-generating process. The protocol adds three more columns to the cross-estimator table.

11. **Bias per parameter.** The mean across replications of the parameter estimate minus the true value. One column per parameter or a summary norm.
12. **Root mean squared error per parameter.** The square root of the mean squared error across replications. Same convention as bias.
13. **Empirical coverage of the 95 percent confidence interval.** The fraction of replications in which the asymptotic confidence interval contains the true parameter. Reported to two decimal places. The target is 0.95. Coverage below 0.90 or above 0.99 indicates a problem with the standard error estimator or with identification.

The number of replications R is reported in the caption. Use R equal to 100 for paper benchmarks and R equal to 1000 for any claim about coverage.

## What to report in every reproducibility footer

Every numerical table or figure in the paper must be accompanied by a reproducibility footer in the caption that names the script that generates it, the random seed, and the hardware. The format is fixed.

> *Generated by `script_path.py` with seed 42 on Apple M2 Pro CPU, econirl 0.0.4, JAX 0.4.30. See replication.md for the full mapping.*

The seed is always 42 unless otherwise noted. The hardware string follows the format used in `econirl.utils.hardware_string` so that it can be regenerated programmatically. The package and JAX versions are pinned at the top of every script.

## What to track but not report

Several diagnostics are tracked in every estimator's `EstimationSummary` but are not displayed in the headline benchmark table. They appear in the appendix, in the documentation, or in error messages when they go out of bounds. The headline table stays clean by reporting only what the reader needs to compare estimators on a single line.

- **Hessian condition number.** Reported in the identification diagnostics block. Flagged in the error log when it exceeds 1e8.
- **Smallest eigenvalue of the Hessian.** Reported in the identification diagnostics block. Flagged when negative or below 1e-6.
- **Number of outer iterations.** Reported in the appendix table for the structural estimators. The CCP family uses NPL iterations, the NFXP family uses L-BFGS iterations.
- **Number of inner iterations.** Reported in the appendix table for the estimators with an inner Bellman solve.
- **Function and gradient evaluation counts.** Tracked in `EstimationResult.num_function_evals`. Useful when comparing optimizers but not needed in the headline table.
- **Pre-estimation diagnostics.** Feature matrix rank, condition number, state coverage, and single-action state count, as required by the project CLAUDE.md before any estimator is run on a new dataset. Reported once per dataset, not once per estimator.

## Required pre-estimation diagnostics

Before any benchmark table is generated on a new dataset, the following four diagnostics must be reported and any failures must be resolved. This requirement is inherited from the root project CLAUDE.md and is non-negotiable.

1. **Feature matrix rank.** Computed as `numpy.linalg.matrix_rank` on the flattened feature matrix of shape `(num_states * num_actions, num_features)`. Must equal `num_features`. A deficient rank means the model is under-identified before any data is observed and the benchmark is meaningless.
2. **Condition number.** Computed as `numpy.linalg.cond` on the nonzero rows of the feature matrix. Values above 1e6 are flagged. Values above 1e9 abort the run.
3. **State coverage.** The fraction of states observed at least once in the panel. Below 0.5 the run aborts. Below 0.8 the run continues with a warning printed to the benchmark log.
4. **Single-action states.** The count of states in which only one action is ever observed. CCP estimators degenerate at the boundary, so any benchmark that includes a CCP estimator must report this count.

The four diagnostics print before the first estimator runs and are stored alongside the benchmark CSV.

## Required ablations for any new estimator

Adding a new estimator to econirl requires a Monte Carlo benchmark on at least three data generating processes before the estimator moves from `econirl.contrib` into `econirl.estimation`. The three data generating processes are the Rust bus engine replacement panel as the canonical one-dimensional structural problem, the gridworld in `econirl.environments.Gridworld` as the canonical small-state-space IRL problem, and the Keane-Wolpin occupational choice panel as the canonical large-state-space problem. The benchmark report compares the new estimator against NFXP, MCE-IRL, and BC as the three reference estimators.

The new estimator passes the production gate when it satisfies five conditions. It converges in the convergence flag column on at least 95 percent of replications across the three data generating processes. Its empirical coverage of the 95 percent confidence interval is between 0.90 and 0.99 on at least one of the three. Its cosine similarity to ground truth on the Rust bus panel is at least 0.99. Its percent optimal value on the gridworld is at least the BC baseline plus three percentage points. Its wall-clock time is reported and falls within an order of magnitude of the comparable estimator in its family.

## Required transfer test for any IRL estimator

Inverse reinforcement learning estimators are tested on transfer because the recovered reward is the deliverable. The transfer test holds out a transition structure that the estimator did not see during fitting and measures the percent optimal value of the estimated reward under the held-out transitions. A reward that does not transfer is a reward that has memorized the dynamics rather than recovered the preferences. The transfer test is required for every estimator labeled as inverse reinforcement learning in the taxonomy table and is reported in the percent transfer value column of the headline benchmark table.

The transfer test on the Rust bus panel uses a perturbed mileage transition matrix in which each transition probability is multiplied by a Beta(2, 2) noise variable and renormalized. The transfer test on the gridworld uses a different obstacle configuration. The transfer test on the Keane-Wolpin panel uses a perturbed wage process. All three perturbations are deterministic given the seed.

## Versioning

Each benchmark CSV is stamped with the package version at the top. The format is `# econirl 0.0.4 generated 2026-04-25 on Apple M2 Pro` as the first line of the file. Any benchmark CSV in the repository older than the current minor version is rerun before the paper is recompiled.

## Failure modes to flag in the benchmark log

The benchmark runner emits a warning, not a silent failure, in any of the following cases.

- An estimator's standard errors are NaN. The cause is almost always a singular Hessian. The fix is to switch to bootstrap standard errors or to add a regularization term.
- An estimator's cosine similarity to ground truth falls below 0.5 with the convergence flag set to true. The estimator has converged to a wrong solution and the warning routes to the maintainer.
- An estimator's wall-clock time exceeds the reference estimator in its family by more than two orders of magnitude. Either the estimator is mis-tuned or the reference is. The maintainer investigates.
- An estimator's percent transfer value falls below its percent optimal value by more than 20 percentage points. The recovered reward is dynamics-specific and the estimator's claim of transferability is false.

## Summary

The headline column set is point estimate, standard error, log-likelihood, time, convergence, cosine similarity, percent optimal value, and percent transfer value. The Monte Carlo extension adds bias, root mean squared error, and confidence interval coverage. Every numerical artifact in the paper carries a reproducibility footer naming the script, the seed, and the hardware. The pre-estimation diagnostics run before any benchmark and any new estimator clears a five-condition production gate before it leaves contrib. These rules are not aspirational. Every benchmark in the paper and in the documentation conforms.
